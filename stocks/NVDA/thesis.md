# NVDA — NVIDIA Corporation

## One-line thesis
NVIDIA compounds at >30% revenue growth through 2027 on AI data-center infrastructure dominance + software lock-in via CUDA, *because* its combination of hardware, software ecosystem, and developer inertia creates switching costs that no competitor has yet replicated at scale — *this thesis is wrong if* AMD or custom silicon (Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Meta MTIA) materially erodes hyperscaler GPU wallet share, or if AI capex cycles abruptly reverse.

## Snapshot
Semiconductor/AI infrastructure · Tech · ~$5.1T cap · founded 1993 · at $210.69 (2026-06-18)

## What it does
NVIDIA designs and sells GPUs and AI accelerators, primarily for data-center training and inference workloads (its Compute & Networking segment), and for gaming and professional visualization (Graphics segment). The H100/H200/Blackwell GPU family is the dominant hardware platform for training large language models and deploying AI inference across hyperscalers and enterprise data centers globally. NVIDIA also makes its CUDA software stack, which has become the de-facto programming environment for GPU compute — creating an enormous developer flywheel that compounds its hardware lead.

## Industry & TAM
AI infrastructure spending is the single fastest-growing capex category globally. Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon) are on track to collectively spend $300B+ on data-center capex in 2026, with GPU/accelerator procurement the largest single line item. Enterprise AI adoption broadens the TAM to industrial, healthcare, and scientific compute. The sovereign AI buildout (every major government buying domestic GPU infrastructure) added another durable demand vector in 2025–2026. Conservatively, the total addressable market for AI accelerators is $200–400B/yr by 2027-2028.

## Why I'm watching
The intersection of a near-monopoly on the most critical resource in AI buildout with an expanding software moat is rare. NVIDIA's forward P/E (~16.5x) has compressed dramatically as earnings have grown into the valuation — making this a more tractable entry than it was at its 2023 peaks. Key question is sustainability of data-center demand and whether the software ecosystem holds as alternatives mature.

## Outlook — Bull / Base / Bear
- **Bull (30%)**: Blackwell demand sustains 40%+ revenue growth through 2027; CUDA flywheel + NVIDIA NIMs/software revenue materially expands margins; sovereign and enterprise AI add a new growth layer. Target $320+.
- **Base (50%)**: Growth moderates to 25–30% as hyperscaler GPU spend normalizes; AMD captures 10–15% share at the margin; margins hold at current levels. Target $250.
- **Bear (20%)**: Custom silicon (Trainium, TPU v5, etc.) accelerates; AI capex cycle contracts sharply after 2026 build-out; AMD/Intel close the software gap enough to pressure pricing. Target $150.

## Moat
NVIDIA's moat is the combination of **process power** (years of GPU architecture iteration, manufacturing partnerships with TSMC on leading nodes, proprietary CoWoS packaging relationships) layered on top of an extraordinary **switching cost** in CUDA. There are roughly 4 million developers writing CUDA code; the installed base of trained models is overwhelmingly in CUDA/PyTorch. Moving workloads to ROCm (AMD) or custom silicon requires months of re-optimization per model. **Scale economies** are real: NVIDIA's R&D budget ($9B+ in FY2025) is 5–10x AMD's, allowing a multi-year lead on each successive architecture. The weak spot: **counter-positioning** is limited — hyperscalers have strategic incentive to reduce NVIDIA dependency, they have the engineers to do it, and Amazon's Trainium external sales push (June 2026 news) is a live threat to the thesis.

## Management & capital allocation
Jensen Huang founded NVIDIA in 1993 and has run it continuously — rare longevity with extraordinary execution. ROIC has expanded from ~20% to 80%+ over 5 years, among the highest in the S&P 500. Share buybacks are active and disciplined ($25B authorized in 2024). M&A track record is mixed: the $40B Arm deal was blocked (2022), Mellanox ($7B, 2020) was an excellent acquisition. CEO compensation is high but performance-aligned. Governance risk is lower than many peers (no super-voting structure).

## Valuation
- **Multiples**: At ~$5.1T market cap on ~$130B FY2026E revenue, that's ~39× sales — still elevated. Trailing P/E 32.3× is actually reasonable given growth; forward P/E ~16.5× implies the Street expects another near-doubling of earnings in the next 12 months.
- **Reverse-DCF**: At $210, the market is pricing in roughly **25–30% revenue CAGR for the next 5 years plus gross margins holding at 70%+**. This is achievable if the data-center cycle remains strong, but it prices in almost no execution risk. There is no margin of safety unless the cycle extends beyond 2027.

## Balance sheet
Strong: $38B cash, minimal long-term debt, robust free cash flow (~$60B+ annualized FY2026). No balance sheet risk. The only flag is capital return timing — large buyback programs can slow during downturns if management wants to preserve optionality.

## Key metrics to watch
- **Data center revenue** (was ~$47B in FY2026 Q1 annualized — trend is the signal)
- **Gross margin** (currently ~75%; compression signals competitive pressure)
- **Hyperscaler capex guidance** — Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, Meta earnings calls
- **AMD MI300X/MI400 customer wins** (canary for CUDA switching)
- **Blackwell backlog + lead times** (a shrinking backlog is the first warning)
- **Custom silicon revenue ramp** (watch AWS Trainium and Google TPU traction)

- Gross margin falls below 65% on competitive pricing pressure
- One or more hyperscalers publicly announces a material shift away from NVDA toward custom silicon for new workloads
- Jensen Huang departure or governance deterioration
- U.S. export controls tighten further in a way that meaningfully impairs revenue
