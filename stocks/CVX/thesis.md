# CVX — Chevron Corporation

## One-line thesis
Chevron is the highest-quality US-listed integrated major — Permian scale, diversified global portfolio, and a fortress balance sheet that supports a durable 4%+ dividend yield and buyback program that makes it a self-liquidating asset at current prices, *because* its low-cost Permian and Tengiz assets produce cash across most oil price scenarios — *this thesis is wrong if* WTI breaks sustainably below $50/bbl, the Hess-Guyana arbitration goes against Chevron, or the energy transition accelerates faster than any credible scenario expects.

## Snapshot
Energy · Oil & Gas Integrated · ~$346B USD cap · $173.63 · 30× trailing P/E (elevated by impairments) · ~14× forward P/E · next earnings 2026-07-31

## What it does
Chevron operates globally in three segments: **Upstream** (exploration, development, and production of crude oil and natural gas — Permian Basin, Kazakhstan's Tengiz field, Gulf of Mexico, Australia LNG, and other international assets); **Downstream** (refining, marketing, and chemicals — ~20 refineries globally, branded fuel stations); and **All Other** (technology, insurance, real estate). The Permian Basin is now Chevron's crown jewel, producing ~900,000 bbl/day and targeting 1M+ by 2027 (post-PDC Energy integration).

## Industry & TAM
Chevron operates in a market with steady demand (103M bbl/day globally) against a supply backdrop increasingly controlled by OPEC+ discipline and US shale. The recent US-Iran peace deal (June 2026) has loosened supply temporarily, pressuring oil prices — CVX is down ~19% from its 52-week high. At ~$65–70 WTI, Chevron generates ~$15B+/yr in free cash flow. At $80 WTI, it's $20B+/yr. The company's guidance assumes $60 WTI for capital planning, implying significant FCF optionality at current prices.

## Why I'm watching
The thesis is capital return discipline — not growth. At $173 and ~4.5% dividend yield, with buybacks running at $3–4B/quarter, the total shareholder return is ~7–8%/yr before any oil price optionality. The Hess acquisition ($53B, pending arbitration over ExxonMobil's ROFR on Guyana) creates uncertainty but also significant upside: Guyana's Stabroek block is one of the best oil discoveries of the past 30 years. The stock is trading at a historically wide discount to its integrated peers, reflecting the arbitration overhang.

## Outlook — Bull / Base / Bear
- **Bull (25%)**: Hess arbitration resolved in Chevron's favor (expected H2 2026); Guyana adds 200K+ bbl/day by 2028; WTI recovers to $80+ on OPEC+ discipline; Permian hits 1M bbl/day milestone. Target $220+.
- **Base (50%)**: Hess closes with Guyana intact; WTI averages $65–70 through 2027; Permian growth continues at plan; capital returns of 7–8% annually. Target $195.
- **Bear (25%)**: Hess arbitration goes against Chevron (loses Guyana ROFR); WTI averages $55–60 on Iran + OPEC+ production increase; California and EU regulatory headwinds increase; Permian growth disappoints on pipeline constraints. Target $140.

## Moat
Chevron's moat is primarily **scale economies** and **cornered resources**. The Permian Basin acreage (100K+ acres in the Delaware and Midland basins, acquired via Anadarko and PDC Energy deals) represents a multi-decade inventory of low-cost wells at $35–40/bbl breakeven. The Tengiz (Kazakhstan) field is one of the largest oil fields in the world with low extraction costs and a 50-year production horizon. The **process power** of a fully integrated operator (own refining, pipelines, and marketing) provides earnings stability when crude prices are volatile. The weak moat components: **no counter-positioning** against renewables (Chevron's clean energy program is modest), and **branding** is largely undifferentiated in the energy sector. The real risk is not competitive — it's regulatory and geopolitical.

## Management & capital allocation
Mike Wirth (CEO since 2018) has been one of the most disciplined capital allocators in Big Oil. Chevron did not overextend in the 2021 commodity cycle, kept debt at conservative levels, and has consistently returned 75–80%+ of operating cash flow to shareholders via buybacks and dividends. The PDC Energy acquisition ($6.3B, 2023) was a clean bolt-on that added Permian scale at attractive prices. The Hess deal ($53B) is the outlier — expensive and complicated by the ROFR dispute with ExxonMobil. Dividend history is pristine: Chevron has raised its dividend for 36+ consecutive years (Dividend Aristocrat). ROIC at ~$70 WTI is ~12–14%.

## Valuation
- **Multiples**: Trailing P/E is elevated at 30× due to 2025 asset impairments. Forward P/E of ~14× is more representative and trades in line with XOM (~13×) and at a slight premium to international majors. Historically, CVX trades at 12–15× forward earnings — current valuation is within the normal range, with upside if the Hess deal closes cleanly.
- **Reverse-DCF**: At $173, the market is pricing in roughly **$62–65/bbl WTI long-run and flat production at ~3.1M boe/day** — essentially no growth credit. If Hess closes and Guyana adds 200K+ bbl/day, the implied price at a 13× multiple on FY2028 earnings is ~$210–220. The current price embeds the worst-case Hess outcome at a conservative oil price, which is a potential mispricing.

## Balance sheet
Strong. Net debt ratio ~19% (comfortable for an integrated major). $5–6B cash on hand. AA-range credit rating. The Hess acquisition adds ~$7B in incremental debt but remains manageable. Chevron generated ~$18B in operating cash flow in FY2025. Dividend is safe at $50/bbl WTI.
