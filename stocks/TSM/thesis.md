# TSM — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company

## One-line thesis
TSM is the irreplaceable chokepoint of the AI hardware stack: every advanced AI chip from NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, and Broadcom runs through TSMC fabs, and the 2nm/1.4nm technology lead makes the moat deeper by the year — *this thesis is wrong if* geopolitical escalation over Taiwan causes customer diversification away from TSM fabs, Intel Foundry closes the process gap within 3–4 years, or AI hardware spending plateaus before TSMC's Arizona/Japan fabs de-risk the concentration.

## Snapshot
Pure-play semiconductor foundry · Technology / Semiconductors · ~$2.4T cap · NYSE: TSM (ADR) · Founded 1987 · Hsinchu, Taiwan

## What it does
TSMC is the world's largest dedicated semiconductor foundry — it manufactures chips designed by others. It makes chips for effectively every significant semiconductor company: Apple (the largest customer at ~25% revenue), NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm, Broadcom, MediaTek, and hundreds of others. TSMC doesn't design chips; it invests billions in process technology to stay at the leading edge of miniaturization and then sells manufacturing capacity. At the most advanced nodes (3nm, 2nm, and the forthcoming N2P and A14/1.4nm), TSMC has only one credible peer in Samsung and no peer at all in yield and customer trust. Its trailing 12-month revenue is approximately $88B; net income is approximately $36B; operating margin approximately 45%.

## Industry & TAM
The global semiconductor industry is ~$600B/yr. Foundry services are approximately $120–130B. At the leading edge (sub-7nm), TSMC's share is ~90%+. AI accelerator demand is the dominant growth driver: Blackwell and successor GPU generations require the most advanced packaging and nodes, and TSMC is the only producer. Taiwan chip export restrictions on China (news from June 9, 2026) add a short-term revenue risk for legacy nodes but actually validate TSMC's strategic position — the world is reorganizing semiconductor supply chains around TSMC's advanced capability, not away from it.

## Why I'm watching
At $462 and ~$2.4T market cap, TSM trades at 40× trailing and ~24× forward earnings. The forward multiple looks reasonable for a company growing revenue 30%+ in 2025. The real question is whether the Taiwan geopolitical risk is priced correctly — the stock has doubled from $206 twelve months ago, and sentiment is euphoric (put/call 1.53, suggesting hedging demand). The Apple-Intel chip deal announced June 18 is a near-term headwind for TSMC (Intel Foundry gaining Apple volumes = less for TSMC), but at current scale, Apple node concentration is modest.

## Outlook — Bull / Base / Bear
- **Bull (35%)**: AI capex cycle extends into 2027–2028; N2/A14 ramp expands margins; Arizona and Japan fabs de-risk geopolitical concerns for US/Japanese customers; ASP expansion on advanced packaging. Target $550–$600.
- **Base (45%)**: 20–25% revenue growth in 2026–2027; margins stable at 44–47%; geopolitical premium persists but doesn't widen; Taiwan Strait remains tense but no escalation. Target $440–$480.
- **Bear (20%)**: Taiwan Strait escalation causes customer hedging and fab investment diversion to Intel/Samsung; AI capex plateau; China export restrictions broaden and hurt legacy node revenue. Target $280–$320.

## Moat
TSMC has one of the strongest moats in technology. The core is **process power**: the accumulated know-how to manufacture chips at 2nm and below is not teachable, not transferable, and not replicable quickly — it represents 35+ years of R&D and tens of billions in capital expenditure. TSMC also benefits from **scale economies**: with 90%+ leading-edge share, it amortizes its process R&D over far more wafers than Samsung, and its yield learning curve is faster. **Switching costs** are profound — chip designers build their products for a specific fab's design rules (PDKs), and switching foundries means re-designing the chip, a multi-year process. **Cornered resource** is the most honest description: without TSMC, the AI hardware stack doesn't exist. No one else can make the most advanced chips at scale. The real risk to the moat is not competition but geopolitics — Taiwan's geography is an asset (exceptional human capital, supply chain, government support) and a liability (Taiwan Strait risk is the single most discussed tail risk in technology investing).

## Management & capital allocation
TSMC was founded by Morris Chang; current CEO C.C. Wei is a 30-year TSMC veteran and widely respected as technically expert and strategically sound. Capital allocation is disciplined: TSMC has maintained a policy of increasing dividends and is executing a multi-hundred-billion USD fab expansion (Arizona, Japan, Germany) funded with a combination of FCF, government subsidies, and debt — without over-levering. ROIC is ~25%+. The concern is not governance but geopolitics: Taiwan's democratic government and TSMC management's alignment with global customers are positives, but neither controls the Taiwan Strait.

## Valuation
- **Multiples**: At $462, trailing P/E ~40× (high) but forward P/E ~24× (reasonable for growth profile). Revenue growing 30%+ in 2025; earnings growing faster due to operating leverage.
- **Peer comparison**: Samsung Foundry and Intel Foundry trade at much lower multiples — but both are structurally inferior businesses. The premium is justified; the question is how much.
- **Reverse-DCF**: At $462, the market is pricing in roughly 20–25% revenue CAGR for 4–5 years followed by stable margins. That's the base case, not a heroic assumption. The stock looks fairly priced to slightly expensive given Taiwan risk — not a screaming buy from here, but defensible as a core position in the AI value chain.

## Balance sheet
Net cash position (cash exceeds total debt). TSMC is self-funding its massive capex program, which speaks to the FCF generation. Total assets ~$200B+, no leverage risk. The major capex commitment (Arizona Phase 2, Japan Fab 2) is large but well within TSMC's cash generation capacity.

## Key metrics to watch
- **Revenue growth rate** (must sustain 20%+ for bull case; 2025 tracking ~30%)
- **Gross margin and operating margin** (gross margin at or above 55% is the goal; below 50% signals node-mix headwinds)
- **N2 / A14 node ramp timeline** (each new node unlocks a new pricing tier and margin expansion)
- **Advanced packaging revenue** (CoWoS/SoIC for AI chips; currently strained supply — a bottleneck for NVIDIA)
- **US/Japan fab yield and cost** (subsidized fabs must demonstrate competitiveness; cost disadvantage vs Taiwan is a margin risk)
- **China legacy node revenue exposure** (currently ~12–15% of revenue; export control expansion risk)

## Disconfirming events (would make me sell)

- AI hardware spending plateau: NVIDIA revenue guide down >20% would reduce advanced node demand materially
- TSMC's Arizona Fab Phase 1 (N4) demonstrates structural cost disadvantage that bleeds into advanced nodes — raises doubt about non-Taiwan diversification economics
- Taiwan export restriction broadens to include advanced packaging, impacting NVIDIA shipments directly
