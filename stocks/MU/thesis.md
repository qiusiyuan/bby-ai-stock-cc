## One-line thesis
Micron is riding an AI-driven memory supercycle: HBM (high-bandwidth memory) for AI accelerators is structurally different from prior DRAM cycles — supply is capacity-constrained rather than oversupplied — and Micron is the only US-headquartered HBM supplier, capturing share as NVIDIA, AWS, and Apple can't get enough — *this thesis is wrong if* HBM supply catches up faster than demand, Micron loses yield parity on HBM4, or a macro-driven inventory digestion cycle triggers a standard DRAM price collapse that overwhelms HBM gains.

## Snapshot
Memory and storage semiconductor maker · Technology / Semiconductors · ~$1.28T cap · NASDAQ: MU · Founded 1978 · Boise, Idaho

## What it does
Micron designs and manufactures DRAM (dynamic random access memory), NAND flash storage, and increasingly high-bandwidth memory (HBM) for AI workloads. The product portfolio spans DRAM (server, mobile, PC, graphics), NAND (data center SSDs, client SSDs, enterprise flash), and HBM (a 3D-stacked DRAM product used in AI accelerators like NVIDIA's H100/H200/Blackwell). Revenue is split roughly 60% DRAM / 40% NAND by product, but HBM is the fastest-growing and highest-margin segment within DRAM, and it is the entire bull thesis in concentrate. The company serves cloud hyperscalers, PC OEMs, smartphone makers (Apple is a major customer), auto, and industrial markets.

## Industry & TAM
The global memory market is ~$120–130B/yr, with HBM currently a small but explosively growing slice. At the AI acceleration layer, each Blackwell GPU rack requires several hundred GB of HBM3E; scaling to tens of thousands of racks means the HBM TAM is growing faster than fab capacity. The conventional DRAM market (PC, mobile) remains cyclical and competitive between Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix. SK Hynix currently has the highest HBM market share (~50%), Samsung (~35%), Micron (~15%) — Micron is ramping aggressively and the US government has incentive to see Micron succeed (CHIPS Act subsidies). The news cycle as of June 2026 confirms an Apple memory cost acknowledgment and a Goldman Sachs fair value estimate lift to $866 — signaling the HBM narrative is not priced out.

## Moat
Micron's moat is weaker than TSMC's but real in specific areas. Memory manufacturing requires **process power** (years of yield optimization, stacking technology, equipment relationships), and Micron is one of only three companies globally that can produce leading-edge DRAM at scale. For HBM specifically, the TSV (through-silicon via) stacking process is technically demanding — not every fab can do it. **Scale** provides some protection but Samsung and SK Hynix are comparable in scale, and Samsung's cost structure has historically been lower. The honest assessment is that memory is a **commodity business with periodic moat moments** — those moat moments occur when supply is structurally constrained (now, due to the shift from commodity DRAM to HBM which is more fab-intensive and requires capacity re-tooling). The risk is that moat moments end when supply catches up, which is the recurring story in memory investing.

## Management & capital allocation
CEO Sanjay Mehrotra has led Micron since 2017 after the SanDisk acquisition (where he was CEO). He pivoted the company toward data center from consumer memory, which was the right long-term call. Capital allocation has been mostly sound: Micron invested aggressively in DRAM/NAND during the cycle downturn (2022–2023) and is now harvesting the upcycle. The CHIPS Act deal (~$6.1B in grants for US fabs in Idaho and New York) secures funding for HBM capacity expansion. Concerns: Micron has historically been a capital destroyer in down cycles (DRAM industry is structurally prone to overinvestment). ROIC is highly volatile (near zero in troughs, 20%+ at peaks). Shareholder returns (buybacks) are secondary to capex during expansion phase.
