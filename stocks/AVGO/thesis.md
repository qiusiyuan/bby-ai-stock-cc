# AVGO — Broadcom Inc.

## One-line thesis
Broadcom is the quiet compounder of AI infrastructure: its custom ASIC business (XPUs for Google TPU, Meta MTIA) is growing faster than GPU alternatives and carries premium margins, while the VMware acquisition creates a durable software cash machine — *this thesis is wrong if* hyperscalers in-house their custom silicon design at scale (cutting Broadcom out), VMware customers defect to open alternatives at a higher rate than expected, or AI infrastructure spend shifts to merchant silicon and away from custom ASICs.

## Snapshot
Semiconductors + Infrastructure Software · NASDAQ · ~$1.96T cap · $411 (2026-06-18) · 52w range $244.17–$495.00

## What it does
Broadcom operates in two segments. **Semiconductor Solutions** designs application-specific chips (custom AI accelerators / XPUs, networking ASICs for ethernet switching, storage controllers, Wi-Fi, broadband chips) for hyperscalers, OEMs, and telcos. **Infrastructure Software** is primarily VMware (private cloud virtualization, NSX networking, VCF subscription), acquired in late 2023 for $61B — the largest tech acquisition in history at the time.

## Industry & TAM
The custom AI ASIC market (XPU) is the highest-conviction secular growth story inside Broadcom. Hyperscalers spend to build training and inference chips tuned to their specific model architectures; Broadcom is the primary design and supply partner for Google (TPU v4/v5) and Meta (MTIA). This market is growing explosively — estimates suggest $25–40B TAM by 2027 as Google and Meta collectively spend tens of billions per year on in-house silicon. VMware's TAM is large ($50B+ virtual infrastructure and private cloud) but growth is slower; the thesis is margin extraction, not rapid growth.

## Why I'm watching
Broadcom is the only semiconductor company simultaneously winning in custom AI silicon, ethernet networking (critical for scaling AI clusters), and enterprise software (VMware). The combination gives it exposure to AI infrastructure capex from multiple angles, with VMware providing FCF stability that NVDA and AMD lack. JPMorgan (June 2026) and Citi both rate it Buy on data center demand. The Architect Labs raise ($24M seed, June 2026) to compete with Broadcom's custom chip business is worth watching — it's the first credible well-funded challenge to the labor-intensive XPU design workflow — but it's seed stage, not a near-term threat.

## Outlook — Bull / Base / Bear
- **Bull (30%)**: Google and Meta XPU programs scale to $15B+ of Broadcom silicon spend by 2027; VMware migrations complete at higher ARPU than feared; ethernet networking wins incremental share from InfiniBand for AI clusters. Target $600+.
- **Base (50%)**: XPU revenues compound 40%+ through 2026–2027; VMware churn contained to <10% of base; ethernet networking grows steadily. Forward earnings support $480–520. Target $480.
- **Bear (20%)**: One or both major hyperscaler XPU customers (Google/Meta) bring design fully in-house or shift to a new partner; VMware enterprise churn accelerates above expectations; AI capex cycle cools materially. Target $280 or below.

## Moat
Broadcom's moat is **switching costs layered with cornered resource**. Once a hyperscaler's silicon team co-designs a custom chip with Broadcom engineers over a 2–3 year cycle, switching is costly in time, IP, and process knowledge. The design expertise Broadcom has accumulated across 10+ generations of hyperscaler custom silicon is genuinely hard to replicate — it is not just an engineering staff, it is institutional process knowledge (why Architect Labs' seed-stage "AI-accelerated chip design" is interesting but not a near-term threat). VMware's switching costs are well-established: enterprise data centers are deeply embedded in vSphere and VCF, and the migration cost to alternative hypervisors or Kubernetes-only stacks is measured in years. The **networking ASIC** business (ethernet switches, NICs) benefits from scale economies — Broadcom's Tomahawk and Trident families are de facto standards in spine-leaf architectures globally.

## Management & capital allocation
Hock Tan has run Broadcom since 2006 with a serial M&A model: acquire businesses with sticky recurring revenue, cut costs aggressively, raise prices on captive customers, and use FCF for buybacks and debt repayment. ROIC track record is exceptional. The VMware acquisition raised leverage to ~4× EBITDA but the company has been deleveraging rapidly; the June 2026 $2.5B tender offer for senior notes is on-strategy. Capital return discipline is strong — Broadcom has been a consistent buyback machine. The main risk with Tan's model is that future M&A targets are increasingly large and potentially harder to integrate.

## Valuation
- **Multiples**: At $411, trailing P/E of 68× and forward P/E of 21× — the spread is large because reported earnings include heavy VMware-related amortization. Adjusted (cash) earnings are substantially higher. ~$1.96T cap on estimated $55–60B FY2026 revenue (semiconductor + VMware).
- **Reverse-DCF**: At $411, the market is pricing in roughly **25–30% revenue CAGR over 5 years with operating margins stabilizing at 55–60% on an adjusted basis**. This is achievable in the base case — Broadcom has delivered this repeatedly under Hock Tan. A Trefis note (June 2026) specifically called out that "the real price tag on Broadcom stock is not what it seems" — meaning forward-year earnings make the stock look much cheaper than trailing multiples suggest. That is accurate: on 2027E earnings, the forward multiple is in the mid-teens, which is fair for a business of this quality.

## Balance sheet
Post-VMware, Broadcom carries ~$70B gross debt. FCF generation is strong enough (~$20–25B/yr) to service and repay meaningfully each year. The leverage is manageable, not dangerous. Deleveraging pace is the key balance sheet metric to watch.

## Key metrics to watch
- **AI/custom ASIC revenue quarterly disclosure** (Broadcom has begun breaking this out — watch for XPU revenue YoY growth)
- **VMware ARR and churn** (success of the subscription transition; any ARR deceleration signals pricing resistance)
- **Adjusted operating margin** (target >60% ex-amortization; any compression signals integration issues)
- **Gross debt reduction pace** (watch against FCF; are they buying back shares or paying down debt faster?)
- **Ethernet networking market share** (AI cluster networking is a direct competition with Nvidia InfiniBand)
- **New hyperscaler XPU customer wins** (rumored Apple XPU engagement — any confirmation is a major catalyst)

## Catalysts (next 12 months)
- **2026-09-03**: Q3 FY2026 earnings — XPU revenue disclosure and VMware ARR update
- **2026–2027**: Apple XPU engagement rumored — any confirmation would be a major re-rate catalyst
- **H2 2026**: VMware subscription migration milestones (year 3 of integration)
- **Ongoing**: Ethernet AI-cluster wins vs. InfiniBand (watch hyperscaler networking architecture announcements)

## Disconfirming events (would make me sell)
- Google or Meta publicly announces a move away from Broadcom for next-generation XPU design
- VMware annual recurring revenue growth turns negative (customer churn accelerates meaningfully)
- Broadcom's adjusted gross margin falls below 65%, signaling mix shift toward lower-value products
- Hock Tan announces retirement with no credible successor named
- A major acquisition at a price that requires >5× EBITDA leverage (signals discipline has broken)
