# INTC — Intel Corporation

## One-line thesis
Intel is a turnaround bet on Lip-Bu Tan rebuilding foundry credibility with the Trump-era tailwind of domestic chip manufacturing mandates and the Apple chip deal as the validation event — *this thesis is wrong if* Intel 18A process technology fails customer qualification, the Apple deal scope is narrow or symbolic rather than volumes-based, or the core PC/server CPU business loses further share to AMD/ARM before the foundry ramp saves it.

## Snapshot
CPU designer + aspiring foundry · Technology / Semiconductors · ~$673B cap · NASDAQ: INTC · Founded 1968 · Santa Clara, CA

## What it does
Intel operates three segments. **CCG (Client Computing Group)** makes desktop and laptop CPUs — this is the legacy cash cow, slowly losing share to AMD and Apple Silicon. **DCAI (Data Center & AI)** makes Xeon server CPUs, data center GPUs (Gaudi), and networking chips — Gaudi is the main AI angle but has struggled to gain traction against NVIDIA. **Intel Foundry** is the turnaround story: Intel is attempting to become a contract chip manufacturer (fabless foundry model), competing with TSMC. Intel Foundry's 18A process node is the keystone — if it works at volume with competitive yield, Intel becomes a second source for advanced manufacturing. The Apple deal announced June 18, 2026 by President Trump (Trump stating "Apple agreed to work with Intel to design and build its Chips in America") is the first major public validation of Intel Foundry's commercial ambitions.

## Industry & TAM
The foundry market is ~$120–130B/yr. Intel wants a meaningful slice, particularly in the US-located advanced-node segment (the $40–50B+ segment that goes to TSMC's Arizona fabs and Samsung's US fabs). The CHIPS Act provides ~$8.5B in grants to Intel (confirmed) plus loan guarantees — this is the largest CHIPS Act award and signals the US government is strategically invested in Intel's success. The CPU market is mature but Intel still holds majority server CPU share (~70%+ Xeon). If Gaudi gains any meaningful AI accelerator share (even 5% of the $80B+ GPU market), that's material upside.

## Why I'm watching
INTC at $134 is up 607% from its 52-week low of $18.97 — an extraordinary move. The stock was a value trap for years. The Apple deal announcement on June 18, 2026 is the sentiment catalyst that has the stock near its all-time record high ($135.48). The critical question is whether this rally is the start of a legitimate turnaround or whether it's Trump-deal euphoria that fades when the details emerge. At ~87× forward P/E, Intel needs the foundry revenue to materialize and the CPU business to stabilize. This is a high-risk, high-reward situation.

## Outlook — Bull / Base / Bear
- **Bull (25%)**: Intel 18A yields prove competitive with TSMC N2 by 2027; Apple volumes are real (200M+ iPhone chips/yr); DCAI Gaudi wins meaningful hyperscaler deployments; foundry revenue ramps to $15B+ by 2028. Target $170–$200.
- **Base (40%)**: Apple deal is real but limited to specific chip types (not iPhones); 18A works but at modest volumes by 2027; server CPU share loss slows under Tan; foundry revenues grow slowly; EPS recovers toward $3–4 by 2027. Target $120–$140.
- **Bear (35%)**: Apple deal scope is symbolic or delayed; 18A struggles with yield (mirrors prior process failures); AMD continues server CPU share gains; DCAI Gaudi fails to monetize at scale; company requires further restructuring. Target $70–$90.

## Moat
Intel's moat has been materially eroded and is being rebuilt. Historically, Intel had the strongest moats in semiconductors — process power (best fabs), scale, and branded franchise (Intel Inside). Those moats weakened: TSMC overtook Intel's process leadership in 2017, and AMD closed the CPU design gap by 2020. What remains: **brand** in enterprise (Xeon has embedded operational trust in data center IT), **scale** in manufacturing (Intel has more US fab capacity than anyone), and a nascent **cornered resource** in government-subsidized domestic advanced manufacturing (the CHIPS Act grants make Intel's fabs strategically important to US national security). The question is whether CEO Lip-Bu Tan can rebuild **process power** — the evidence is early but promising (18A is the first genuinely competitive process Intel has shown since 2015). Apple's willingness to design chips for Intel Foundry would be definitive confirmation; the details matter enormously.

## Management & capital allocation
Lip-Bu Tan (former Cadence CEO) joined as Intel CEO in March 2025. He is well-regarded in the semiconductor industry and is executing a lean-and-focused restructuring: workforce reduction (~15% headcount in 2024–2025), capex discipline, and foundry-first strategy. The governance situation is complicated by US government ownership (~10% stake via CHIPS Act deal mechanisms) — creates alignment on domestic manufacturing but potential political interference on strategic decisions. Capital allocation is in transition: dividends were cut in 2023; buybacks paused. The priority is foundry capex and technology recovery, not shareholder returns. This is appropriate for a turnaround but means the investment is in future earnings, not current returns.

## Valuation
- **Multiples**: At $134, trailing P/E is not meaningful (recent losses), forward P/E ~87× — extremely high for a semiconductor company in turnaround. This price assumes successful foundry execution.
- **Peer comparison**: AMD trades at 40–50× forward; TSMC at 24×. Intel at 87× forward only makes sense if foundry revenues and margins ramp sharply — a multi-year process.
- **Reverse-DCF**: At $134, the market is pricing in roughly $4–5 EPS by 2028 and foundry revenues growing from near-zero to $10B+. That's the bull case. The current price has essentially zero margin of safety — it prices successful execution of a difficult turnaround at scale. Buying here is paying for the outcome, not the process.

## Balance sheet
Total debt ~$50B; cash ~$25B — net debt of ~$25B, significant for a company with volatile earnings. CHIPS Act grants partially offset capex, but Intel's fab investments are enormous. The leverage is manageable if turnaround executes; it becomes a pressure point if foundry ramp is delayed another 2 years. No near-term liquidity crisis, but the balance sheet is tight enough that any major setback would limit strategic optionality.

## Key metrics to watch
- **Intel 18A customer qualification progress** (the gating factor; any named customer win beyond Apple validates the thesis)
- **Foundry external revenue** (must reach $5B+ by 2027 for bull case)
- **Server CPU market share** (Xeon vs AMD EPYC; any stabilization or share gain is positive)
- **Gaudi AI accelerator sales** (currently sub-$1B; $3B+ by 2027 would indicate real AI traction)
- **Gross margin trajectory** (must recover toward 50%+ from current ~40%; sign of foundry maturation)
- **Workforce and cost structure** (Tan's restructuring must hold without impairing R&D)
