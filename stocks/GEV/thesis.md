# GEV — GE Vernova

## One-line thesis
GEV is the sole at-scale supplier of gas turbines and grid infrastructure at the exact moment AI-driven power demand is breaking a 20-year plateau in US electricity consumption — *this thesis is wrong if* power demand growth disappoints (hyperscaler capex retreats), GEV fails to expand gas turbine margins above the mid-teens, or the wind segment's losses drag the company into a cash deficit that forces dilution.

## Snapshot
Power equipment, grid infrastructure & energy transition · Industrials (Specialty Machinery) · ~$298B cap · NYSE: GEV · Spun off from GE, April 2024 · Cambridge, MA

## What it does
GE Vernova was carved out of General Electric in April 2024 and operates three segments. **Power** (the profit engine) makes gas turbines, nuclear steam turbines, and hydro generators — GEV has roughly 7,000 gas turbines installed globally, representing ~30% of the world's electricity generated from gas. **Electrification** provides grid solutions: high-voltage substations, transformers, power conversion, and grid software — this segment is the fastest-growing and highest-margin business today, directly tied to AI data center buildout and grid hardening. **Wind** (the drag) makes onshore and offshore wind turbines; offshore wind has been deeply loss-making and management is restructuring it aggressively (targeting break-even by 2026). The Power and Electrification segments are what investors own; Wind is the liability that management is working to cure.

## Industry & TAM
US electricity generation has been essentially flat for 15 years. That plateau is now breaking: AI data centers are expected to add 50–100 GW of new US demand by 2030 (various utility estimates), and grid decarbonization requires massive substation and transformer investment. GEV is positioned at both choke points — they make the gas turbines that will bridge supply during the transition, and the grid hardware that enables new transmission. The global transformer market alone is $60B+/yr with backlogs extending to 2027–2028. Gas turbine order books are equally stressed. This is a multi-year structural demand cycle, not a single quarter of orders.

## Why I'm watching
GEV is a rare case of an industrial company with legitimate pricing power in a supply-constrained market. You cannot spin up new gas turbine manufacturing capacity quickly — GEV has 78,000 employees and a global service network built over decades. The Bernstein Outperform initiation at $1,206 (June 18, 2026) and the Iran reconstruction opportunity both represent upside not yet in consensus estimates. The backlog is growing faster than revenue, which means the forward earnings picture is improving even as the current price already reflects significant re-rating since the $479 52w low.

## Outlook — Bull / Base / Bear
- **Bull (30%)**: Power segment margins expand to 20%+ as turbine pricing holds; Electrification grows 25%+ driven by data center and grid spending; Wind reaches break-even. Target $1,400–$1,500.
- **Base (45%)**: Power margins stable at 15–17%; Electrification 15–20% growth; Wind approaches but doesn't reach break-even by 2026; earnings grow 25–35% in 2025–2027. Target $1,100–$1,200.
- **Bear (25%)**: Power demand growth disappoints; hyperscalers defer data center buildout; Wind losses continue and require management distraction / write-downs; re-rates to 25× forward earnings. Target $700–$750.

## Moat
GEV's moat is narrower than it looks but real in specific verticals. In **gas turbines**, the installed base of 7,000 units creates powerful switching costs — utilities are locked into GEV's service contracts because the turbines are already there, generating long-tailed, high-margin service revenue. Process power is also present: GEV's H-class turbine technology represents 20+ years of iterative engineering that competitors (Siemens Energy, Mitsubishi) cannot shortcut. In **grid infrastructure**, the backlog situation is almost cartel-like — transformer lead times are 2+ years, and GEV has pricing power it hasn't used in a decade (now using it). Counter-positioning exists against pure renewables plays that can't offer dispatchable power. The weakness: no meaningful moat in wind (it's a competitive equipment market), and long-term gas turbine demand has a terminal risk from electrification and decarbonization — offset by the near-to-medium-term reality that AI power demand isn't waiting for clean energy.

## Management & capital allocation
CEO Scott Strazik took GEV public and has focused sharply on margin expansion and Wind turnaround. He has signaled clearly that Wind restructuring is the priority over growth. Post-spinoff, GEV established a clean balance sheet with minimal debt. No track record yet on buybacks (too new), but the dividend initiation and capital return framework announced in 2025 signals confidence in FCF generation. The company has ~$8B in cash/short-term investments and minimal debt — fortress balance sheet for an industrial. ROIC is expanding from near-zero in 2023 toward the low teens as Wind losses shrink and Power/Electrification margins improve. The governance structure is straightforward (no super-voting, standard S&P 500 board).

## Valuation
- **Multiples**: At $1,110, GEV trades at ~32× trailing earnings (distorted by Wind losses) and ~45× forward earnings. These look expensive relative to industrial peers at 20–25× forward. But earnings are in a step-up phase — consensus 2027 EPS is roughly 2× the 2025 number.
- **Peer comparison**: Siemens Energy trades at 30–35× forward; Emerson at 22×; GEV premium is justified by growth profile but not unlimited.
- **Reverse-DCF**: At $1,110, the market is pricing in roughly 20–25% revenue growth for 3–4 years and operating margins expanding from ~7% today toward ~14–15% by 2028. The margin expansion assumption is plausible given Power pricing and Wind improvement, but the timeline is tight. This is a "good company, high expectations" situation — not a screaming buy, but defensible if execution holds.

## Balance sheet
~$8B cash with minimal long-term debt. Net cash position is exceptional for an industrial of this size. No leverage risk. The concern is not the balance sheet; it's Wind segment cash burn (~$1B+ annual operating loss at peak), which is being absorbed by Power/Electrification profits. If Wind resolution takes longer than 2026, ongoing losses are a drag but not existential given the cash cushion.

## Key metrics to watch
- **Power segment margin** (watch for expansion toward 18–20%; currently ~15%; the core thesis driver)
- **Electrification revenue growth** (must sustain >15–20% to justify premium multiple)
- **Wind segment operating loss trajectory** (path to break-even; quarterly losses must narrow)
- **Gas turbine and grid backlog level** (current: ~$115B; any deceleration would be concerning)
- **Service revenue as % of Power revenue** (high-margin locked-in; should grow to 60%+ over time)
- **Put/call ratio** (currently 1.28 — elevated; market is hedging the run)

## Catalysts (next 12 months)
- Q2 2026 earnings — July 22, 2026: first post-Bernstein-initiation print; margin expansion commentary key
- Iran reconstruction: as US-Iran deal progresses, GEV is a major beneficiary of Persian Gulf grid rebuild (gas turbines, transmission) — potential for large international orders
- Wind restructuring milestones: any announcement of capacity reduction, asset sales, or break-even pull-forward
- US grid investment policy: Infrastructure Act implementation, utility capex updates
- New data center power agreements: hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon) signing long-term power agreements directly with GEV or its customers accelerates the demand narrative

## Disconfirming events (would make me sell)
- Hyperscalers announce material pullback in data center capex (demand driver reversal)
- Wind segment losses in 2026 exceed 2025 — break-even timeline slips to 2028+
- Power segment margin guidance cut below 13% — indicates pricing pressure or cost inflation overwhelming improvement
- Competitor (Siemens Energy, MHI) takes significant market share in a major gas turbine tender
- US policy shift significantly favors renewables + storage, deferring gas turbine investment
